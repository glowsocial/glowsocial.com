# Glow Social SEO/AEO Operating Manual

This is not a diary. It is the current operating manual for Glow Social SEO/AEO work.

A learning belongs here only if it changes the next page, bet, internal-link pass, or conversion edit. Generic observations, one-off outcomes, and unexplained failures do not belong here.

## Current strongest principles

### 1. Buyer intent beats broad traffic

Prioritize pages where the reader is already comparing costs, tools, services, alternatives, or the burden of doing social media themselves. Broad educational traffic is useful only when it naturally routes to a buyer-intent page.

Good targets include:

- social media management cost
- affordable social media management
- tool alternatives and comparisons
- “should I hire or DIY” questions
- “I do not want to do social media” owner-burden questions
- pages where a preview can answer the reader’s uncertainty

### 2. The real pain is the owner still carrying the content burden

For Glow Social, the strongest answer is rarely “we schedule posts.” The sharper answer is that most tools, freelancers, and DIY workflows still leave the owner deciding what to say, writing captions, finding ideas, and restarting after the calendar goes quiet.

When editing a page, identify what the competing option still leaves on the owner’s plate.

### 3. Preview-first CTAs are the best bridge

Prefer CTAs that let the reader see posts from their own website before committing.

Use language like:

- “See posts from your website first.”
- “Preview what Glow would create from your site.”
- “Check the posts before you choose another tool.”

Avoid making the primary bridge “book a demo” unless the page’s intent clearly calls for sales contact.

### 4. Direct answers should appear before tools, tables, or long explanations

For AEO and search snippets, a page should answer the buyer’s question in visible copy near the top. Calculators and comparison tables are stronger when preceded by a short direct answer that frames how to interpret the result.

For cost pages, include the unpaid owner-time cost before the calculator or plan table.

### 5. Comparison pages should explain the leftover job, not just compare features

For alternatives and “vs” pages, lead with the job the reader still needs done. Explain what the compared tool is good at, then name what it does not solve for a busy local business owner.

The useful distinction is usually:

- scheduling tool: organizes posts after someone creates them
- agency/freelancer: can help, but often expensive or meeting-heavy
- DIY AI: produces drafts, but still needs prompting, editing, calendar discipline, and publishing
- Glow Social: prepares posts from real business context for review and approval

### 6. Learn mostly from winners; treat losers carefully

Do not turn every underperforming page into a rule. A page can underperform because of indexing delay, weak internal links, poor execution, low search demand, SERP competition, canonical issues, or timing.

When a page fails, log uncertainty. Do not rewrite the operating manual unless the evidence is clear.

### 7. Narrower rules are better than broader rules

A useful learning should be specific enough to affect the next edit.

Weak:

> Comparison pages are important.

Useful:

> On comparison pages, lead with the work the competing tool still leaves to the owner, then offer a preview-first next step. Feature parity tables alone are not enough for Glow’s buyer.

## Current page-type patterns

### Calculator/tool pages

Treat an interactive calculator as decision support, not the whole page. Surround the tool with:

1. direct answer above the calculator
2. interpretation guidance below it
3. adjacent internal links to pricing, benchmarks, alternatives, or service pages
4. FAQ/schema matching visible copy
5. preview-first CTA when the result reveals that owner time is still the hidden cost

### Pricing/cost pages

Do not only compare subscription prices. Include:

- direct monthly cost
- owner time cost
- content creation burden
- what is included vs. still left to do
- where a lower-cost done-for-you option fits

### Alternatives/comparisons

Use the structure:

1. What the other tool is good for
2. What it still leaves to the owner
3. Who should still choose it
4. Who needs Glow instead
5. Preview-first CTA

### AEO question pages

Answer quickly, then explain the tradeoff. Link to the most relevant canonical buyer page. Avoid generic “it depends” answers unless followed by a practical decision rule.

## Rules for updating this file

Update this file only during a weekly learning review or when a very clear durable learning appears.

When updating:

1. Rewrite the relevant section instead of appending a dated note.
2. Remove rules that are too generic, contradicted, or based on unclear failures.
3. Prefer fewer, sharper rules.
4. Keep only guidance that changes the next SEO/AEO action.
