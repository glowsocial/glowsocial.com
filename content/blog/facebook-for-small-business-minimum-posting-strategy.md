Most Facebook guides tell you to post daily, engage with every comment, run Facebook Ads, go live weekly, and join every relevant group.

That's great advice for a full-time social media manager. For a small business owner who's also the accountant, the HR department, and the person who fixes the toilet when it breaks — it's unrealistic.

Here's the **minimum viable Facebook strategy** that keeps your page looking professional without consuming your life.

## The Only Facebook Metric That Matters for Small Business

Forget followers, reach, and engagement rates. For most small local businesses, Facebook serves one purpose: **proof of life.**

When a potential customer Googles your business, finds your Facebook page, and sees recent posts — they trust you more. When they see a page that hasn't posted in 3 months, they wonder if you're still in business.

Your goal isn't to go viral. It's to look active and legitimate.

## The 4-Post Rotation (Never Run Out of Ideas)

Rotate through these 4 post types and you'll never stare at a blank screen:

### 1. Behind-the-Scenes (35% of posts)
Show the human side of your business. This doesn't require photography skills:
<ul>
<li>Photo of your workspace, team, or daily routine</li>
<li>"Here's what we're working on this week"</li>
<li>Stories about why you started your business</li>
<li>Before-and-after of a project or service</li>
</ul>

### 2. Helpful Tips (25% of posts)
Share knowledge your customers find valuable:
<ul>
<li>"3 signs you need [your service]"</li>
<li>Seasonal tips related to your industry</li>
<li>Common mistakes your customers make (and how to avoid them)</li>
<li>Answers to questions you get asked constantly</li>
</ul>

### 3. Customer Stories (25% of posts)
Social proof builds trust:
<ul>
<li>Customer testimonials (with permission)</li>
<li>Google review screenshots</li>
<li>"We helped [customer] with [problem]" stories</li>
<li>Thank-you posts for loyal customers</li>
</ul>

### 4. Promotional (15% of posts)
Only 15% should directly sell:
<ul>
<li>Current specials or limited offers</li>
<li>New service or product announcements</li>
<li>"Book now for [season]" reminders</li>
<li>Link to your website or booking page</li>
</ul>

## The 2-Hour Monthly Batch Method

Don't try to post in real-time. Instead:

<ol>
<li><strong>Block 2 hours</strong> on the first of each month</li>
<li><strong>Create 12 posts</strong> (3 per week x 4 weeks) using the rotation above</li>
<li><strong>Schedule all 12</strong> using Facebook's built-in scheduler or Buffer (free)</li>
<li><strong>Walk away</strong> — your Facebook is handled for the entire month</li>
</ol>

Total time: 2 hours per month. Compare that to the 10-15 hours most guides recommend.

## Common Facebook Mistakes Small Businesses Make

<ul>
<li><strong>Posting only promotions</strong> — Followers tune out. Keep promos to 15% of posts.</li>
<li><strong>Going dark for weeks</strong> — Inconsistency is worse than low frequency. 1 post/week beats 10 posts one week and nothing for a month.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring their page entirely</strong> — An empty Facebook page actively hurts you. Customers assume you're closed.</li>
<li><strong>Over-investing early</strong> — You don't need Facebook Ads, Reels, Lives, and Stories from day one. Start with the minimum and expand only if Facebook is driving business.</li>
<li><strong>Comparing themselves to big brands</strong> — Starbucks has a social media team. You don't. Your standard is "active and professional," not "viral."</li>
</ul>
