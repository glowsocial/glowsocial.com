Before you invest in social media tools—or automated posting like Glow Social—it's worth understanding what social media can and can't do for a local business. Spoiler: It's usually not the biggest lever for getting customers, but it does play an important supporting role.


## What Social Media Is Good At

<strong>Building familiarity:</strong> When someone needs your service, they're more likely to choose a business they've been seeing in their feed for months. Social media builds the "I feel like I know them" factor.


<strong>Staying top of mind:</strong> Between purchases, social media keeps you visible. When customers need you again—or when a friend asks for a recommendation—you're the name that comes up.


<strong>Showing your work:</strong> For visual businesses (restaurants, salons, contractors), social media is a running portfolio. Potential customers see what you actually do.


<strong>Building trust:</strong> Consistent posting signals a real, active business. Inconsistent or abandoned profiles signal the opposite.


## What Social Media Is Bad At

<strong>Immediate customer acquisition:</strong> Most people don't scroll Instagram thinking "I need a plumber right now." For immediate needs, they search Google. Social media is the long game, not the instant win.


<strong>Replacing word-of-mouth:</strong> For most local businesses, referrals remain the #1 source of new customers. Social media supplements referrals—it doesn't replace them.


<strong>Driving sales without trust:</strong> Someone who just found you on social media probably isn't ready to buy immediately. They need to see you multiple times before taking action.


## Where Social Media Fits in the Customer Journey

<strong>Awareness:</strong> People discover you exist (through posts, shares, or ads)

<strong>Consideration:</strong> They follow you and see your content over time

<strong>Trigger:</strong> They need what you offer (or a friend asks for a recommendation)

<strong>Decision:</strong> You're top of mind because of consistent visibility

<strong>Purchase:</strong> They reach out or visit


Social media primarily works in the awareness and consideration phases—building familiarity that pays off later.


## Realistic Expectations for Local Businesses

<strong>What to expect:</strong>

<ul>
<li>Gradual increase in awareness over 6-12 months</li>
<li>Occasional direct inquiries from followers</li>
<li>Referral boost (people who recommend you point to your social profiles)</li>
<li>Professionalism signal (active profiles reassure potential customers)</li>
</ul>

<strong>What NOT to expect:</strong>

<ul>
<li>Flood of customers after posting for a month</li>
<li>Viral moments that transform your business</li>
<li>Complete replacement of other marketing channels</li>
<li>Immediate ROI you can measure in sales</li>
</ul>

## Is Social Media Worth It for Local Businesses?

Short answer: Yes, but keep the investment proportional.


<strong>Social media makes sense when:</strong>

<ul>
<li>It doesn't cost too much time or money relative to its impact</li>
<li>You can maintain it consistently long-term</li>
<li>You have realistic expectations about what it does</li>
</ul>

<strong>Social media doesn't make sense when:</strong>

<ul>
<li>It's consuming hours you should spend on higher-impact activities</li>
<li>You're expecting immediate, measurable sales</li>
<li>You can't maintain consistency (inconsistent profiles hurt more than help)</li>
</ul>

## The Priority Order

For most local businesses, prioritize in this order:


<ol>
<li><strong>Deliver great service</strong> (creates referrals)</li>
<li><strong>Google Business Profile</strong> (captures "near me" searches)</li>
<li><strong>Ask for reviews</strong> (builds trust and helps Google ranking)</li>
<li><strong>Consistent social presence</strong> (maintains visibility, builds familiarity)</li>
</ol>

Social media is #4—important but not the foundation.


## What Consistent Social Media Does Over Time

After 6-12 months of consistent posting:


<ul>
<li>People recognize your business name</li>
<li>When someone asks for recommendations, you come to mind</li>
<li>Potential customers check your profile and see an active business</li>
<li>You have a running record of your work and personality</li>
<li>Some followers become customers—not as a direct result of one post, but from accumulated familiarity</li>
</ul>

None of this shows up in week one. It accumulates.
